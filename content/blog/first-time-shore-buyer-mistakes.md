Buying a shore home is not like buying a primary home — it just looks like it. The rhythms are different (whole blocks trade in spring), the costs are different (hello, flood insurance), and the emotions are *much* louder, because nobody falls in love with a starter ranch the way they fall in love with a porch two blocks from the ocean.

After 27 years at the Jersey Shore, Andrea has watched the same seven mistakes repeat. Consider this your vaccination.

## 1. Budgeting for the price, not the carry

The listing price is the entry fee; taxes, insurance, flood coverage and salt-air maintenance are the membership dues. Buyers who skip this math fall in love with houses they will resent by February. We wrote a full breakdown of [what a shore home really costs each year](/blog/cost-of-owning-a-jersey-shore-home) — read it before you tour anything.

## 2. Skipping flood diligence

Two houses on the same street can carry wildly different flood premiums depending on elevation and construction year. **Ask for the elevation certificate and the seller's current flood policy up front.** If the answer is a shrug, that is information too.

## 3. Treating the inspection as a formality

Coastal homes fail differently: corroded fasteners, wind-worn roofing, moisture where winter closed the house. Hire an inspector who works *at the shore*, and be present for the walk-through. An hour on site teaches you more about the house than any report.

## 4. Ignoring the HOA and master insurance on condos

The classic Ocean City duplex-condo is a beautiful way in — five bedrooms of shore house at a condo price. But the association's master insurance decides what *you* must insure, and its reserves decide your next special assessment. Read both documents before the offer, not after.

## 5. Shopping only in summer

July is when you fall in love; **spring is when you buy well.** Serious inventory lists between February and May, and the buyers who toured in March had their pick. By the time the boardwalk is full, the best houses are under contract — check what is [already spoken for](/collection) and you will see the pattern.

## 6. Underestimating rental logistics

"We'll just rent it peak weeks" is a plan, not a system. Licensing, turnovers, linens, cleaners on Saturday changeover — the towns with the strongest demand also have the most competitive rental operations. If income matters to your math, say so early; some blocks and floor plans rent dramatically better than others, and Andrea will tell you which are which.

## 7. Using an agent who doesn't live the market

The shore market runs on block-level knowledge: which streets flood in a nor'easter, which builder's decks age well, what the identical house sold for in April. A generalist from an hour away is negotiating blind. This is precisely why [how we work](/about) starts with the market, not the paperwork.

## The short version

- Know the full annual carry before you tour
- Flood documents before offers
- Shore-specialist inspector, and be there
- Read condo master policies
- Shop in spring, close before summer
- Treat rental income as a designed system
- Hire block-level expertise

## FAQ

### When is the best time of year to buy at the Jersey Shore?

Late winter through spring, when serious listings arrive and summer competition hasn't. Closing before Memorial Day means your first season in the house is the summer you bought it for.

### Is a shore condo a good first move?

Often, yes — especially Ocean City's two-unit buildings, which deliver real square footage with shared expenses. The diligence shifts to the association's documents.

### How much down payment do lenders want on a second home?

Second-home financing typically asks more down than a primary residence — commonly in the 10–20%+ range depending on the lender and profile. Model scenarios with the payment calculator on any [listing page](/properties).

### Do I really need a local agent if I already found the house online?

The photos found the house; a local agent finds the truth about it — flood history, block dynamics, and what it should actually trade for. That difference is usually worth multiples of the commission.
