Ask what a shore house costs and everyone quotes you the price. Ask what it costs to *own* one, and the room gets quiet. The truth is that a Jersey Shore home carries a second, smaller mortgage made of taxes, insurance and salt air — and the buyers who budget for it up front are the ones who never resent their house.

Here is the honest breakdown, line by line.

## 1. The mortgage itself

At today's typical 30-year fixed rates, financing is usually the largest single line. On a $2.5M home with 20% down, principal and interest alone generally lands well into five figures per month. Every listing on this site includes a [payment calculator](/properties) so you can slide the down payment and rate yourself — a half-point of rate moves the number more than most people expect.

**Pro move:** shore buyers with flexible cash often model both 20% and 40% down. The second scenario frequently turns a "stretch" into a comfortable carry.

## 2. Property taxes — better than the NJ horror stories

New Jersey is famous for the highest property taxes in America, and statewide the average *effective* rate runs north of 2%. Here is the surprise: **the shore towns are the exception.** Resort communities like Ocean City spread their budgets across a huge second-home tax base, so effective rates typically land closer to the ~1% neighborhood — often less than half of what the same buyer pays in North Jersey.

On a $2.5M home, that difference is tens of thousands of dollars a year. It is one of the quietest arguments for shore ownership, and almost nobody markets it.

## 3. Homeowners insurance

Coastal wind exposure means shore premiums run higher than inland New Jersey. Budget roughly **0.3–0.6% of the home's value per year** as a planning range, and expect wind/hurricane deductibles to be a percentage of dwelling coverage rather than a flat number. Newer construction with current wind mitigation (impact glazing, modern roof tie-downs) prices meaningfully better — one more reason the new builds on the island command what they do.

## 4. Flood insurance — the line nobody skips

If the home sits in a FEMA flood zone (much of the barrier islands does), your lender will require flood coverage. Two things every shore buyer should know:

- **Elevation is everything.** A home built high on pilings to modern code can carry a surprisingly reasonable premium; an older grade-level cottage in the same zone can cost multiples more.
- **Ask for the seller's elevation certificate and current policy** before you write the offer. It is the fastest way to know the real number instead of the scary hypothetical.

## 5. HOA and condo fees

Single-family homes on the island usually have no HOA at all. Condos and the classic Ocean City "two-family" buildings typically do — anywhere from modest shared-insurance arrangements to full-service buildings with pools. Always read the master insurance policy: what the association covers determines what you must cover.

## 6. Maintenance — the ocean's rent

Salt air is beautiful and corrosive. Realistic owners budget **1–2% of home value annually** across:

- Exterior washdowns, paint and trim (salt spray is relentless on the ocean side)
- HVAC service — coastal units live harder lives
- Deck sealing and railing hardware
- Winterization and spring opening if you close the house off-season

## 7. Utilities and the off-season

A house that sleeps ten in July still heats itself in January. Second-home owners typically keep systems at minimum settings off-season, but the shoulder bills are real. If you plan to rent peak weeks, strong summer rental demand in towns like Ocean City and Wildwood Crest historically offsets a meaningful slice of annual carry — many owners aim for July–August bookings to cover taxes and insurance for the year.

## The bottom line

A useful rule of thumb for the barrier islands: **all-in annual carry beyond the mortgage often lands between 2% and 3.5% of the home's value**, depending on elevation, age and how hands-on you are. On a $2.5M home, plan for that range, verify the specifics on the actual house — and then enjoy the fact that you knew the real number before you signed.

## FAQ

### Do all Jersey Shore homes require flood insurance?

Only homes in FEMA-designated flood zones with a mortgage are *required* to carry it — but on the barrier islands that covers a great deal of the housing stock, and even outside required zones many owners choose coverage. The premium depends heavily on elevation.

### Are property taxes really lower at the shore than the rest of NJ?

Effective rates in resort towns like Ocean City typically run well below the New Jersey state average because the tax base includes so many second homes. Always confirm the actual current tax bill on any specific property.

### Can summer rental income cover my carrying costs?

In strong rental towns, peak-season weeks have historically covered a meaningful portion of taxes and insurance for many owners. It depends on the home, the block and how many prime weeks you are willing to give up — Andrea can model it street by street.

### What surprises first-time shore owners the most?

Almost always maintenance: the pace at which salt air works on paint, metal and mechanicals. Budget for it from year one and it never becomes a surprise.
